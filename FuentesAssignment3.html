What A Wonderful World

Authors: Bob Thiele and George David Weiss

Louis Armstrong's "What A Wonderful World" primarily features a simple arrangement with a focus on Armstrong's iconic vocals. The instrumentation includes a trumpet, accompanying strings, a rhythm section with drums and double bass, and occasional piano chords. The trumpet, played by Armstrong himself, takes a prominent role, delivering the uplifting melody. The orchestration creates a timeless and soothing atmosphere, emphasizing the song's optimistic lyrics about the beauty of the world.

Chimes Blues

Authors: King Oliver and Willie Cornish

This traditional jazz piece, typically features instruments like cornet, clarinet, trombone, piano, banjo, double bass, and drums. The cornet takes the lead with expressive melodies, complemented by the clarinet's agile runs and the trombone's rich harmonies. The banjo provides rhythmic support and adds a distinctive sound. The piano contributes with chords and occasional solos, while the double bass and drums establish the steady rhythm. The combination of these instruments creates the lively and energetic atmosphere characteristic of early jazz, capturing the essence of the genre's improvisational and collective spirit.

Snake Rag

Authors: Joe "King" Oliver

This classic jazz composition, typically features a lineup of instruments common in traditional jazz bands. The ensemble includes cornet, clarinet, trombone, piano, banjo, tuba or double bass, and drums. The cornet leads with lively melodies, accompanied by the clarinet's intricate lines and the trombone's harmonic support. The banjo provides rhythmic drive, while the piano adds chords and occasional solos. The tuba or double bass maintains the low end, and drums contribute to the lively rhythm. Together, these instruments create the spirited and infectious sound of traditional jazz, capturing the essence of the genre's improvisational and rhythmic elements.

Armstrong's cornet leads with expressive improvisations, complemented by the clarinet and trombone's lively contributions.
